Reflection (250–500 words)

When I started this class, my Spanish lived in scattered sticky notes—random words, a few phrases, and not much confidence. Now I can actually build a thought, deliver it, and be understood. The three samples on this page map that change pretty well. The “Three Benefits of Being Bilingual” artifact shows I can synthesize ideas and explain them clearly. The independent translation (my Valentina paragraph) proves I can push through a whole thought without stopping at every word. And the Spanish schedule forced me to get comfortable writing time in words and keeping the grammar tidy.

A few skills made the difference. First, “chunks”: small, reusable phrases that keep me from freezing. Second, writing and speaking with time in words made me practice rhythm and structure in a real context. Third, pronunciation—especially the silent “h” and consistent “ch”—became a secret cheat code. As my sounds got cleaner, my listening got less stressful. Contrary to my initial expectations, pronunciation wasn’t just polish; it actually unlocked comprehension and confidence.

Octagonal thinking was a surprise MVP. Looking at a task through eight angles—language, culture, pronunciation, grammar, context, audience, purpose, and self‑evaluation—stopped me from seeing assignments as boxes to check. It turned each piece into a mini‑project where I asked: who am I talking to, what am I trying to do, and how will I know I did it well? That mindset shift made practice feel practical.

I still hit walls. My instinct is to translate word‑for‑word, which makes everything sound robotic. To fix that, I’ve been leaning on chunks, reading my lines out loud, and recording quick voice notes to catch awkward phrasing. I also built simple bilingual templates so I can focus on meaning instead of formatting.

Next steps are concrete: expand my chunk library for common situations (travel, stores, emergencies), practice a few core verb tenses inside short stories, and add 5–10 minutes of daily listening. I’ll keep doing weekly check‑ins with the octagonal lens. Goal: hold a three‑to‑five‑minute conversation without a script and feel comfortable enough to improvise when life throws curveballs—in Spanish.
